The phase is called <strong>Acknowledge.</strong>

It works like this. Before you say a single word about your product, your offer, your price, or your link, you acknowledge that the lead just made a move — typed three words at a stranger on the internet. That move took some non-zero amount of nerve. You name the move, briefly, the way a doctor would acknowledge a patient's nervousness before reaching for the stethoscope. Then you ask one diagnostic question.

That's the entire phase. Three sentences. Maybe four.

Here's the template.

<em>"Hey [name]. Glad you reached out — most people sit on a question like that for a week before they ask. Quick one before I tell you anything: what specifically pulled you to message me? Was it [specific thing in the short] or something else?"</em>

Three things doing work in that opener.

<strong>One — you used their name.</strong> Not <em>"hey friend"</em> or <em>"hey there."</em> A name. The lead is a person and you're treating them like one.

<strong>Two — you acknowledged the difficulty of the move they just made.</strong> Most leads have, in fact, sat on the question for several days before sending it. Naming that out loud lowers their nervous system before the conversation begins.

<strong>Three — you asked a diagnostic question that cannot be answered with <em>yes</em>, <em>no</em>, or a link click.</strong> The question forces the lead to say something specific about their own situation, which is what you'll need in Phase Two (Qualify) to know whether the treatment is even appropriate.

Notice what you didn't do.

You didn't send the link. You didn't name the price. You didn't drop the pitch. You didn't say <em>"let me know if you have any questions."</em> You acknowledged, you diagnosed, you handed control back to the lead. You ran the doctor's opening, not the salesman's.

That's Phase One. Run it tonight on your next inbound. The lead's response will tell you which way to take Phase Two.
