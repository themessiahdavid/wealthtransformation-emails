The objection is The Think-About-It Stall.

It works like this.

Step 1 — Acknowledge.

"Totally fair. This is a real decision and you should think about it. Most people who succeed in this take a moment before they commit."

Notice what you didn't do. You didn't argue. You didn't list reasons not to think about it. You didn't ask "what specifically do you need to think about?" in a way that signals impatience. You acknowledged. The acknowledgment is the entire move.

Step 2 — Reframe.

"Quick question that might save you some thinking time though. When people say 'I need to think about it,' usually one of two things is happening. The first is they're genuinely missing a piece of information they need before they can decide. The second is they've already decided no and they're being polite. Both are valid. Which one is closer to where you are right now?"

This is the key move and it's the move most closers don't make. You name the two options out loud. You give the prospect explicit permission to choose "I've already decided no and I'm being polite." Most prospects who were on the second option will admit it the moment they're given permission. The ones who were on the first option will tell you the specific information they were missing.

Step 3 — Evidence.

If they say "missing information" — "Got it. Tell me what's missing and let's clear it now. Most of the time the missing piece is something I can answer in two minutes."

If they say "already decided no, being polite" — "I appreciate the honesty. Most people don't tell me that and we waste each other's time on a follow-up call neither of us wants. Can I ask what specifically tipped you to no, just so I'm not running this same conversation wrong with the next person?"

Either way, the conversation becomes real instead of becoming a follow-up that dies.

Step 4 — Re-anchor.

If they identified missing information and you've answered it: "Now that piece is clear, where are you on it? Same place, leaning yes, or leaning no?"

If they identified a real no: "Understood. One last thing — you told me earlier this matters because of [their stated why]. If you're not the right fit for this, fine. But the why doesn't go away. What's your plan for handling it instead?"

This is the deeper move. You're not pressing on the sale. You're caring about the why they came in with — the thing that won't be solved by walking away from this offer either. Sometimes this surfaces a real concern that wasn't actually about your product. Sometimes the prospect realizes they have no plan B and circles back. Sometimes they walk and you wish them well.

Step 5 — Permission.

"Either way, no pressure on me. Decide tonight or decide tomorrow. I'd rather you make the right decision than the fast one."

Permission to decline is what makes the close clean. The prospect feels respected. The closer keeps their integrity. The deal that closes here closes because the prospect chose it without feeling pushed.
