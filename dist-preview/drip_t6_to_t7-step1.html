Seven years of chasing. Two minutes of being chased.
 

David — story.

In 2002 I was twenty-one years old, in Phoenix, Arizona, with no warm market, no friends in the city, and a network marketing company that handed me one piece of training as my entire onboarding.

An audio cassette. Prospecting for the 21st Century.

The strategy on the cassette was simple. Walk up to strangers. Deliver the script. Collect business cards. Follow up by phone.

I did the work harder than anyone around me. Thirty approaches before lunch. Forty after dinner. A stack of business cards on the kitchen counter every night. A hand cramp from writing names down.

I made nothing.

For seven years I did versions of this work. Different companies. Different scripts. Same posture every time. I was the chaser. The prospect was the chased. Every conversation started with me trying to convince someone who hadn't asked to be convinced.

By year five, the voice in my head was loud.

By year seven, it wasn't whispering. It was telling me, plainly, that the problem wasn't the work. The problem was me.

I want you to feel where I was at the end of those seven years.

The kitchen table at two in the morning. The room dark. The blue light of the laptop screen on my face. I'd open the contact manager and stare at the names of people I was supposed to call back. People who hadn't returned the last three calls. People who had stopped answering when they saw my number on caller ID.

The hum of the laptop fan. The silence of a house where nobody else was awake. Sometimes my own voice, reading the next line I was supposed to say, just to hear something out loud.

The pressure in my chest. The way my shoulders wouldn't drop, even when I leaned back.

The phone, sitting on the table, somehow heavier than it had been the day before.

Then in 2009 — broke, exhausted, almost out of this profession entirely — I tried something different.

I built a single web page.

The page had a video on it. The video was just me, talking the way I'm writing to you right now, about the architecture of producing in this profession. Below the video was a form. Three fields. Name. Email. Phone number.

I put the page online and I waited.

The first person to opt into that form was a woman from Phoenix.

When I called her — when I, finally, called her, on a number she had given me — before I could say my name, before I could deliver any version of the seven-year-old script — she said:

"Oh my God, it's that guy from the internet."

Two minutes later her credit card was on file.

Two minutes.

After seven years of trying to convince strangers who hadn't asked to be convinced, two minutes of confirming a buyer who had already decided.

Same operator. Same person. Two minutes that took everything I knew about this profession and turned it inside out.

I had become the chased.

Read that again.

It wasn't a better script. It wasn't a better pitch. It wasn't a different mood I was in that morning. It was the architecture underneath the conversation.

The video on the page had pre-framed me. The form had qualified the buyer. The promise of a personal call had set the expectation. By the time the phone rang, the woman from Phoenix had already done all the work I would have spent a chaser's call trying to do.

The architecture had produced the inversion.

The Big Idea is two words.

Architecture, not tactics.

Same hands. Same hours. Same effort. Different funnel. Different outcome.
