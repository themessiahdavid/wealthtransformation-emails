David — story.

Last October I sat down at my kitchen table around eleven at night and posted a short.

I'd been thinking about a single idea all day — a specific reframe most creators get wrong about who their content is actually for — and I wanted it out of my head before bed. So I shot it in one take, uploaded it, brushed my teeth, and went to sleep.

The next morning I checked.

Forty-seven views. One comment. From a guy whose profile picture made him look like he'd just been woken up by a phone call he didn't want.

I read the comment. Closed the tab. Made coffee.

Then I did something I had been doing for about six weeks at that point. I opened a notebook and rewrote the short — not the content, the order.

The original opened with a piece of context, then made the point, then offered an example, then asked a question. Context, point, example, question. That's how most people who think they're "writing tight" actually write. It feels orderly. It dies on the feed.

The rewrite opened with the consequence — the thing that happens if you get the idea wrong — then the reversal, then the proof, then the invitation. Same words, mostly. Same idea, exactly. Different order.

I shot the new one before lunch. Posted it. Closed the laptop. Went on with the day.

By evening it was at 80,000 views.

By 72 hours later it was at 280,000 views and 312 new people had landed on my email list.

Same camera. Same face. Same lighting. Same me. Same idea.

The only thing that changed was the order.

Read that again.
