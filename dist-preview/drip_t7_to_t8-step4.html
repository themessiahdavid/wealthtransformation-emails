The math on why telling them again doesn't work — and the move that does.
 

Here's a piece of pedagogy I'll give you tonight. Use it tomorrow.

When you taught your team something on your last training call — a tactic, a principle, a script, anything — they retained between four and six percent of it. Not what they remember the gist of. What they could actually deploy if you put them on a phone call thirty seconds after the training ended.

Four to six percent.

I've measured this against my own teams more times than I'd like. The number doesn't move much. The variable isn't the team's intelligence. The variable isn't your delivery. The variable is the medium. Direct exposition — here is the principle, here is how to apply it, take notes — produces about four to six percent retention at the deployable level, in the first telling, every time.

Most operators in this profession respond to that number by training more. Same medium. Repeat. Hope the repetition raises the percentage.

It doesn't. The percentage is a structural feature of direct exposition. Repeating direct exposition gets you four to six percent of four to six percent. The math compounds in the wrong direction.

Here's the move that does work.

Embed the principle inside a story.

If you want your team to internalize the rule that the prospect's first objection is rarely the real one, don't say the prospect's first objection is rarely the real one. Tell them about the time you got "I don't have the money" from a guy who was about to drop two thousand four hundred on a fishing boat the next weekend. Tell them what he actually meant. Tell them what landed. Let the principle sit inside the story so the team has to re-derive it on the way out of the story.

Story-embedded training retains in the forty to sixty percent range across my measurements. An order of magnitude better than direct exposition. Same training time. Same operator. Different medium. Different result.

That's tonight's move. Pick one principle you've been trying to get your team to internalize. Find a story in your own life that the principle sits inside. Run the principle through the story instead of directly. Watch what's still in the room next week.
