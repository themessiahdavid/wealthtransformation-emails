In 2002, I had no money. I was living in Phoenix, Arizona, and there was an event in Long Beach, California I had been told I had to be at. The kind of event that, if you were serious about this profession, you went to. My car barely ran. The hotel was out of reach. I asked a friend if I could ride in the back of his car. I asked another friend if I could sleep on the floor of his hotel room. Both said yes. I hitchhiked the second leg.

I made it to Long Beach.

I walked into an auditorium with fifteen thousand people in it. The first event I had ever been to. A friend leaned in and nudged me with his elbow as a man walked out on stage — a small man — and said: "That guy has made over a billion dollars in this industry."

His name was Bill Britt. He had two million active people in his organization. Not two million people. Two million active people. He talked about a vision he had carried for thirty years. The hair on my arms stood up. I paced in the back of the room and said something to myself that became the orientation of the next decade of my life.

Someday. Somehow. Some way. I am going to be on that stage.

That was 2002.

Ten years later — 2012 — I stood on a stage in front of three thousand five hundred people. I was not in the back of the room. I was the man on the stage. The audience was wrapped in a story.
